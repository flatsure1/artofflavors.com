Beurre Blanc

France's most beloved butter sauce — silky, tangy, and endlessly versatile. Once you understand the emulsion, it becomes second nature.

Prep10 min
Cook20 min
Total30 min
Serves4
LevelIntermediate
🥩

Beurre blanc is the cornerstone of classic French sauce-making. Unlike a hollandaise that relies on egg yolks for emulsification, beurre blanc is a pure butter emulsion — a seemingly impossible sauce that holds together through patience, temperature control, and the magic of cold butter.

The secret lies in the reduction. A sharp, acidic base of white wine and shallots concentrates to a thick glaze, creating the scaffolding that the cold butter binds into. Work too fast, let it get too hot, and the emulsion breaks. Work slowly, add butter cube by cube while keeping the pan just warm enough, and you'll achieve something extraordinary.

"A great beurre blanc is not made in a hurry. It is coaxed, whispered to, and persuaded."

Once mastered, this sauce pairs beautifully with pan-seared fish, steamed asparagus, or any delicate protein that needs a bright, butter-rich complement.

The Method

01

Build the Reduction

In a small, heavy-bottomed saucepan, combine the dry white wine, white wine vinegar, and finely minced shallots. Bring to a simmer over medium heat and reduce until nearly all the liquid has evaporated — you should have about 2 tablespoons of thick, jammy shallot glaze remaining. This concentrated base is the foundation of your sauce.

02

Add the Cream (Optional)

For a more stable emulsion, add 2 tablespoons of cold heavy cream to the reduction and reduce by half again. This is a classical technique that gives the sauce more body and makes it more forgiving. Purists omit this step, but for beginners it's a useful safety net.

03

Mount the Butter

Reduce heat to the lowest setting. Begin adding cold, cubed butter one piece at a time, whisking constantly and waiting until each piece is fully incorporated before adding the next. The sauce should never come to a full boil — you want it at a gentle 60–70°C. Keep moving the pan on and off the heat as needed to maintain this temperature.

04

Season & Strain

Once all the butter is incorporated and the sauce is glossy and coats the back of a spoon, season with fine sea salt and white pepper. For a refined presentation, strain through a fine mesh sieve to remove the shallots — or leave them in for a more rustic style. Serve immediately, or hold in a warm bain-marie.

05

Plate & Serve

Spoon generously over seared fish, steamed vegetables, or grilled scallops. A small squeeze of lemon just before serving brightens the sauce beautifully. Finish with fresh chervil or a few drops of high-quality olive oil for an additional layer of flavour.

Ingredients

  • 250gCold unsalted butter, cubed
  • 120mlDry white wine
  • 60mlWhite wine vinegar
  • 3Shallots, finely minced
  • 2 tbspHeavy cream (optional)
  • to tasteFine sea salt
  • to tasteWhite pepper
  • ½Lemon, for finishing

Chef's Notes

Keep butter cold before use — the temperature contrast is what creates the emulsion. If the sauce begins to look greasy, remove from heat immediately and whisk in an ice cube to cool it down.
